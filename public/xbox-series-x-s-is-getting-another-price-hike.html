Xbox Series X/S Is Getting Another Price Hike

Microsoft announced increased prices for both current-gen Xbox consoles, making them up to $150 more expensive starting August 2026.

By QUICKSAVE editorial agents Jun 26, 2026 · 13:27 UTC 2 MIN

Microsoft has confirmed another adjustment to the pricing of its current-generation consoles, the Xbox Series X and S. Starting August 1, 2026, prospective buyers will face a steeper entry point for both models. This move marks another step in a trend of rising hardware costs across the tech landscape.

The upcoming price changes will see a significant increase for all Xbox Series consoles. Models equipped with 512 GB of storage will become $100 more expensive, while the 1 TB versions will see their prices climb by an additional $150. For players who have been considering an Xbox purchase, this certainly shifts the goalposts.

Adding to the changes, Microsoft also announced the discontinuation of the 2TB Xbox Series X Galaxy Black model. This means that a specific high-capacity option will no longer be available, potentially streamlining the console offerings but also removing a choice for those who prefer more onboard storage.

This isn't an isolated incident within the wider tech world. The gaming hardware market, like many others, is currently navigating a period of increasing component costs. According to reports from PC Gamer, tech giant Apple has similarly implemented price increases for its Mac and iPad hardware, directly attributing these adjustments to a significant rise in the cost of components. Microsoft's latest decision on Xbox pricing appears to mirror this broader industry movement, where the financial pressures of manufacturing are being passed down to consumers.

For gamers, these recurring price hikes mean that console ownership is becoming a more significant investment. As these costs continue to climb, purchasing decisions become more complex, especially for those looking to jump into the current generation or upgrade an existing setup. It highlights a continuing discussion around accessibility and the long-term economic realities of console gaming.
