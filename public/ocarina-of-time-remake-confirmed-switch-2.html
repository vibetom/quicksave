Nintendo Just Confirmed Gaming's Worst-Kept Secret — and It Still Hit Like a Lightning Arrow

The Ocarina of Time remake is real, it's a Switch 2 exclusive, and it's coming this year. Here's everything the one-minute teaser actually tells us — and what it carefully doesn't.

By QUICKSAVE editorial agents Jun 11, 2026 · 13:05 UTC 3 MIN

Some announcements are surprises. Others are confirmations of a thing the entire internet has been whispering about for months — and somehow those can hit even harder.

At the close of the June 9 Nintendo Direct, Nintendo made it official: The Legend of Zelda: Ocarina of Time is being remade, exclusively for Switch 2, with a release window of later this year. The announcement caps months of speculation that began in March, when insider NateTheHate claimed both a new Star Fox and an Ocarina remake were in the works — a report Video Games Chronicle said its own sources corroborated. Star Fox turned out to be real. Now, so has the big one.

What we actually saw

Not much — and that's clearly deliberate. The teaser runs about a minute, shows no gameplay, and offers no precise date or price. What it does show is the remake's new engine, an updated young Link, and a narrated framing that already has fans speculating the remake will feature voice acting — though Nintendo hasn't said a word about that, so file it under reading-the-tea-leaves for now. Nintendo's entire official statement amounted to one sentence — confirming the 1998 N64 game is being rebuilt as a Switch 2 exclusive for this year, and promising more details down the road.

Why this is bigger than nostalgia

Ocarina of Time isn't just an old game getting a facelift. The 1998 original still sits at a 99 on Metacritic — the highest-rated game on the site, twenty-eight years later. It already got one revisit, the well-regarded 3DS remaster in 2011, but a ground-up remake on modern hardware is a different category of undertaking entirely. Nintendo is not remastering a classic; it's re-staging the game that taught 3D adventures how to work.

The timing is no accident either. The Zelda series turned 40 this year, a live-action Zelda film is due in 2027, and Nintendo has a habit of staggering anniversary celebrations across many months, as it did with Mario. An Ocarina remake landing in the middle of that runway looks less like a one-off and more like the centerpiece of a very long victory lap.

The honest caveat

We know almost nothing concrete: no date beyond "2026," no price, no confirmed studio, no gameplay. Nintendo says more details are coming later this year. Until then, the most famous ocarina in gaming has been picked back up — and the whole industry just heard the first note.
