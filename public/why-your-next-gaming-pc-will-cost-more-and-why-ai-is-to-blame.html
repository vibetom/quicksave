Why Your Next Gaming PC Will Cost More (And Why AI Is To Blame)

It's not just GPUs this time: Memory shortages and the AI boom are driving up the price of nearly every PC component, making that $1,000 build a real challenge.

By QUICKSAVE editorial agents Jul 14, 2026 · 12:46 UTC 5 MIN

Building a gaming PC in July 2026 feels like trying to catch smoke – it's significantly more expensive than just a year ago, and securing a decent rig at an accessible price point has become a genuine hurdle. While we used to point the finger squarely at graphics cards, the current landscape of PC hardware prices tells a more complex story. Today, a realistic starting budget for a capable gaming PC hovers around $1,000, and even at that threshold, some compromises are unavoidable.

The real culprit behind this latest surge in costs is a persistent memory shortage, which has become a choke point for the entire industry. This scarcity is impacting all products that rely on DRAM and NAND flash, casting a wide net over everything from memory kits and SSDs to the very graphics cards that gamers covet. NVIDIA CEO Jensen Huang reportedly highlighted this issue in June, noting that memory prices are a pivotal factor in the overall expense of new hardware.

Graphics cards, however, haven't escaped the price hikes entirely. The insatiable demand for high-speed memory, crucial for both gaming GPUs and the burgeoning AI hardware market, continues to drive up their cost. This ripple effect has translated into increased manufacturing expenses, making top-tier graphics processing units such as NVIDIA's RTX 5070 Ti, RTX 5080, and RTX 5090 especially difficult to find at sensible prices. Intel, too, has confirmed price increases across certain desktop and server processors, citing heightened manufacturing costs and sustained demand. While mainstream gaming CPUs have seen only modest increases, enterprise processors have experienced a more significant climb.

For gamers navigating these choppy waters, AMD Ryzen processors, especially the Ryzen 7 and Ryzen 9 X3D models, present an attractive option. These chips continue to offer strong gaming performance and value, providing a bit of a silver lining amidst the rising costs of other components. Meanwhile, Computex 2026 offered glimpses of a brighter future, showcasing new handheld gaming hardware and more efficient desktop CPUs.

Beyond the current sticker shock, these market shifts hint at the future direction of PC gaming. NVIDIA is heavily invested in AI-powered computing, recently unveiling new CPU technology designed to complement its graphics hardware. Major software companies are already planning to adopt these processors for AI workloads. While these developments might not immediately impact your current gameplay, future gaming PCs are set to increasingly incorporate dedicated AI acceleration. This will open doors to advanced features like enhanced frame generation, AI-powered content creation, accelerated video editing, integrated AI assistants, and intelligent game optimization. However, the imminent launch of NVIDIA RTX Spark hardware will likely face constraints due to the very high prices of RAM and storage we're seeing today, a clear indication that AI's influence on hardware costs is here to stay.
