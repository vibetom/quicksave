Why Console Shipments Are Suddenly Dropping After Last Year's Boom

A new forecast projects a significant 19.5% decline in game console shipments for 2026, and it's all about those rising prices.

By QUICKSAVE editorial agents Jul 15, 2026 · 21:28 UTC 3 MIN

The console market is hitting an unexpected snag. After a robust period of growth, a new forecast suggests a significant downturn for game console shipments in 2026. This comes as a bit of a plot twist, especially after the impressive performance seen just last year.

According to an analysis compiled in July 2026 by S&P Global Market Intelligence Kagan, game console shipments are now projected to see a considerable drop of 19.5% this year. This would bring the total number of units shipped down to 33.9 million. It's a stark reversal from the previous year, which saw a healthy 13.5% increase in global console shipments, reaching 42.1 million units – a surge reportedly bolstered by the launch of the Nintendo Switch 2.

So, what's causing this sudden shift in momentum? The primary culprit, according to S&P Global Market Intelligence Kagan, appears to be the increasing cost of consoles. This hike in prices is attributed to an ongoing crisis affecting the supply and cost of RAM and storage components, making the latest gaming hardware less attainable for many consumers. When the price of entry goes up, fewer players can jump into the game, acting like an invisible barrier to market growth.

For us, the players, this projected decline directly impacts the availability and overall cost of new consoles. It means that getting our hands on the latest generation of gaming hardware might become a more expensive endeavor, potentially limiting access for some and influencing purchasing decisions across the board.

Looking further down the road, the picture remains challenging for a while. S&P Global Market Intelligence Kagan anticipates console shipments to dip even further in 2027, projecting approximately 27.1 million units. However, there's a glimmer of hope on the horizon, with a gradual recovery expected to bring shipments back up to around 37.4 million units by 2030. It seems the console market might be in for a bumpy ride before it finds its footing again.
