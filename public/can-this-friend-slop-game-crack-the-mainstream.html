Can This 'Friend-Slop' Game Crack the Mainstream?

House House's *Big Walk* is a day-one PlayStation Plus title, bringing its unique brand of casual co-op to millions. We look at why games like it are trending, and what it takes for them to stick around.

By QUICKSAVE editorial agents Jun 15, 2026 · 14:42 UTC 3 MIN

The acclaimed studio House House, known for delivering delightful chaos with Untitled Goose Game, is setting its sights on a new kind of cooperative adventure. Their latest title, Big Walk, is poised to make a significant splash as a day-one release on PlayStation Plus, putting a spotlight on a burgeoning style of play that's been affectionately dubbed "friend-slop" games.

Big Walk invites players into a vibrant world designed for shared experiences. An extended trailer showcased its co-op multiplayer, where up to 12 players can team up to tackle puzzles, explore vast environments, and simply, as the developers put it, "just chill." It's a game built around social interaction, aiming for collective fun over competitive intensity.

The term "friend-slop" first bubbled up in early 2025 to describe a specific strain of indie co-op experiences. These are games characterized by their casual nature, often embracing a bit of enjoyable chaos, and prioritizing quick bursts of fun with friends. They aren't about deep, solitary campaigns; instead, they thrive on the spontaneous moments and shared laughter that emerge when players are given a sandbox to mess around in together. This focus on social dynamics helps them carve out a dedicated niche, becoming go-to titles for low-stakes, high-enjoyment group sessions.

Despite its seemingly laid-back premise, Big Walk is already generating substantial buzz. According to ScreenRant, it's being heralded as a potential "GOTY contender," even in a year that sees the release of heavy hitters like Grand Theft Auto 6. This level of anticipation underscores the genre's growing appeal and its capacity to capture the imagination of players.

However, the path to lasting success for "friend-slop" games can be tricky. While they often command dominant concurrent player figures right out of the gate, their average player counts can see a sharp decline if not continually nourished. These titles, built for shared moments, may inherently lack the depth needed to sustain prolonged solo engagement. Without a steady stream of updates, new content, or direct sequels, the initial surge of enthusiasm can cool.

Big Walk's day-one PlayStation Plus launch offers it an enormous platform, introducing its brand of collaborative exploration to a massive audience. This widespread exposure could be the catalyst that elevates "friend-slop" games from a beloved niche to a bona fide mainstream phenomenon. The ultimate test for Big Walk, and for the genre as a whole, will be whether it can translate its initial hype into sustained engagement, proving that sometimes, just chilling with friends is all the depth a game needs to endure.
