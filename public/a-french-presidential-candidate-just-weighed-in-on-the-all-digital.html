A French Presidential Candidate Just Weighed In On The All-Digital Future Of Gaming

Jean-Luc Mélenchon's comments on GTA 6 and Sony's physical media exit bring player ownership concerns to the political stage.

By QUICKSAVE editorial agents Jul 03, 2026 · 21:39 UTC 3 MIN

The ongoing debate over an all-digital future for video games has reached a new level, moving beyond player forums and industry discussions to the political arena. Jean-Luc Mélenchon, a French presidential candidate, has publicly weighed in on Sony's strategic shift away from physical PlayStation game discs and the digital-only release of Grand Theft Auto 6.

Mélenchon views video games as vital "cultural assets" that warrant legal protection. His core concern centers on the consumer experience of "paying without ever owning anything." This sentiment reflects a common apprehension among players regarding digital content, specifically the inability to loan, resell, or guarantee perpetual ownership of purchased games.

His comments are precisely timed, referencing the anticipated digital-only launch of GTA 6 in 2026 and Sony's announced plan to cease physical disc sales for its games by 2028. These specific examples ground the political conversation in the tangible shifts reshaping the gaming landscape.

This political intervention arrives amidst a palpable wave of frustration from players regarding Sony's digital-only pivot. The controversy has sparked widespread anger among gamers, and even prompted satirical responses from other companies. Halo Studios, for example, conspicuously highlighted the physical disc release for Halo: Campaign Evolved, a clear counterpoint to the industry's digital tide.

Mélenchon's involvement could signal a potential turning point. Elevating player ownership concerns to the political stage might spur broader discussions or even legislative action concerning consumer rights in the digital age. This makes the story one with potential long-term implications, not just for France, but as a precedent for digital ownership debates globally.

His remarks underscore a growing international conversation about what true 'ownership' means when games exist primarily as digital licenses. This echoes past anxieties that have surfaced with the closure of digital storefronts, like the PlayStation 3's, where access to previously purchased content became a genuine concern. The future of gaming, it seems, is now a matter for more than just gamers.
